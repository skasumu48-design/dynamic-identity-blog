Introduction: My Defining Word

If I had to pick a single word to describe myself, it would be dynamic.

To me, being dynamic means constantly evolving, adapting, and learning. I seldom stay static‚Äîwhether it's my opinions, goals, or worldview. Instead, I am perpetually in motion, embracing new experiences and perspectives.

This trait didn't develop randomly. It stems from a cultural narrative that has profoundly influenced how I view myself and my role in a diverse world: my family's migration story from Lagos, Nigeria to Toronto, Canada.

The Story: Moving, Adapting, Becoming

My parents grew up in Lagos, Nigeria, where everything was familiar‚Äîlanguage, food, traditions, and community. However, economic hardships and limited opportunities forced them to make a life-altering decision: leave their homeland and migrate to Toronto, Canada.

"The move was profoundly challenging. My childhood memories are filled with stories of mixed emotions: excitement intertwined with fear, hope balanced with doubt."

In their new country, my family had to adjust swiftly. They learned a new language, navigated unfamiliar employment landscapes, and maneuvered through a society that often didn't fully appreciate or understand their culture.

Preserving Roots While Growing New Branches

Yet, they also clung to significant traditions‚Äîfood, festivals, stories, and values‚Äîthat kept their roots alive. What makes this story truly significant for me is not just the physical move, but how my family refused to view identity as fixed. They didn't cease being who they were; instead, they added new layers to their identities. They became dynamic‚Äîanchored in one culture while evolving to embrace another.

Personal Connection: Why This Makes Me Dynamic

Growing up with this story, I realized that change isn't something to fear but something to embrace. Watching my family adapt while remaining true to themselves taught me that identity can develop and change fluidly.

Living Between Worlds

At school, I often switch between various groups and expectations. At home, I speak Yoruba or follow specific Nigerian customs. With friends, I use different slang, listen to different music, or communicate in English with a Canadian accent. I don't feel the need to select a single version of myself. Instead, I see these shifts as integral parts of my dynamic identity‚ÄîI can belong to multiple spaces and still remain authentic.

  • At School: Engaging with diverse perspectives, adapting communication styles
  • With Friends: Code-switching between cultures seamlessly
  • In Challenges: Drawing on family resilience to adapt and overcome

This migration narrative has fundamentally influenced how I approach challenges. When confronted with something new or difficult‚Äîwhether it's a challenging class, a cultural misunderstanding, or a significant life change‚ÄîI think of my family adapting to entirely new systems, from climate to culture. If they could navigate such profound changes, I believe I can adjust to my own circumstances.

Equity: Different Storms, Different Starting Points

My family's experience taught me about equity in visceral terms. When they moved to Canada, they didn't start on equal footing with others. They faced barriers: accents that marked them as "foreign," credentials that weren't immediately recognized, and significantly less social capital than those already established in Canadian society.

Growing up with these stories, I realized an important truth: people don't all start at the same point, even if they have equal effort or talent. Equity means recognizing these differences and offering support tailored to individual needs, rather than assuming "everyone has the same chances."

Key Insight: My dynamic identity drives me to examine systems critically‚ÄîI look at policies, attitudes, and institutions to see how they either support or hinder people like my family. Credential recognition programs and language training initiatives represent equity in action.

Diversity: Many Stories, Many Identities

Being dynamic allows me to genuinely value diversity. My family's experience is just one narrative among countless others. At school and in my community, I interact with people from diverse backgrounds, religions, languages, and family histories. Instead of feeling overwhelmed by these differences, I find them fascinating.

I understand that just as my migration story has shaped me, their stories‚Äîwhether of Indigenous resilience, refugee survival, or multi-generational Canadian experiences‚Äîhave equally influenced who they are. Being dynamic means viewing culture as constantly evolving rather than fixed or "pure."

Key Insight: This perspective keeps me open to learning from others. I understand that diversity isn't just a checkbox or an ideal, but a tangible reality that brings new ideas, traditions, and perspectives into our communities, making them richer and more innovative.

Inclusion: Making Space for Changing Identities

Finally, my story and my word are deeply rooted in inclusion. Inclusion goes beyond simply allowing people to join in‚Äîit involves creating environments where people can show their authentic selves and embrace their evolving identities without fear of judgment or rejection.

Having grown up navigating multiple cultures, I understand what it's like to code-switch and adapt myself to fit in. I also recognize how significant it feels when someone makes genuine space for my background, stories, and differences.

Key Insight: Being dynamic means I embody that same openness toward others. In group settings, I notice who seems quiet or out of place. When someone offers a different perspective, I listen attentively without dismissing it. My awareness of my own multifaceted, evolving identity makes me conscious that people are never just one thing‚Äîthey are always in the process of becoming, just like I am.

Conclusion: A Moving Identity

My word, dynamic, goes beyond just a personality trait‚Äîit reflects a worldview that sees identity as something that evolves and responds to the world around it.

The story of my family's migration from Lagos to Toronto illustrated that change can be difficult, yes, but it can also be empowering. It demonstrated that we can adjust and grow without sacrificing our core selves.

Key Takeaways

  • Equity involves addressing unequal starting points and systemic barriers
  • Diversity means valuing the many stories and perspectives around us
  • Inclusion is about creating space for everyone's evolving identity

My word and my story influence how I see myself, and more importantly, how I work toward fostering a more understanding, equitable, and inclusive world. As I continue to evolve‚Äîthrough education, experiences, and encounters with diverse perspectives‚ÄîI carry forward the lesson my family taught me: that being dynamic isn't about losing who you are, but about becoming more fully yourself.
